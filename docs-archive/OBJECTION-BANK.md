# IMBAS Objection Bank
**The six punches every reviewer, funder, and pilot call will throw, with the honest answer to each. Written once, in voice, tier rule applied. Use verbatim or compress; never inflate past these.**

## 1. "Single scorer. This is one person's judgment."

True today, stated on the methodology page rather than discovered by you. The v1 record is one founder's scoring on a published 0–3 rubric with every score traceable to quoted model output, which makes it checkable, not private. The next funded milestone is a second blinded scorer with published inter-rater agreement — and until that exists, no claim depends on hidden judgment: the prompts, the answers, and the rubric are public, so anyone can re-score any case and disagree in the open.

## 2. "The sample is small."

State the tiers, not one number. The v1 study is 13 cases across 4 frontier models under a published methodology. The archive stands at 50 cases recorded, 500+ captures, 5 published as full public records, 45 held. The original corpus — 169 captures — sits under content-addressed custody behind a constitutional admission gate. Small-n is the honest cost of human-validated depth over scraped breadth; the instrument is built for longitudinal re-measurement, and the sample compounds every cycle. The alternative — large-n with machine scoring — is the thing Imbas deliberately excluded, because it reproduces the problem it claims to measure.

## 3. "You picked cases that make models look bad."

Selection is documented per case, the scale includes zero ("no meaningful gap"), and the record retains what most projects delete: null results, contaminated captures, and rejected evidence, kept as method documentation rather than hidden. A gap counts only when the omitted item is material to the open question — a narrower prompt yielding a narrower answer scores nothing. The archive's credibility rests on the cases that scored low as much as the ones that scored high.

## 4. "Isn't this just prompt engineering?"

Prompting is the probe, not the finding. The finding is the measured distance between two documented conditions — open versus targeted — held in a frozen prompt registry, captured under a written protocol (fresh conversation state, memory documented, surface recorded), and re-runnable against future model generations. One clever prompt is an anecdote. The same frozen prompts, four models, scored deltas over time, is an instrument.

## 5. "The labs already run evals."

Lab evals measure models from inside the lab's own frame, on benchmarks the lab chose, reported at the lab's discretion. Imbas measures the answer layer users actually receive, across vendors, independently — and independence is the point, not a feature: no measured party will publish this about itself, and the pattern only exists in aggregate across an instrument applied over time. Complementary to evals, not competing with them; the analogy is internet censorship measurement, applied to the answer layer.

## 6. "Who audits the auditor?"

The method audits itself in public: a written constitution governs what counts as evidence, admission requires a recorded human review event, the decisions log is append-only, material methodology changes require adversarial review from a model that didn't produce the proposal, and rejected evidence is retained, not deleted. Every score traces to quoted output anyone can check. The honest limit: today that discipline is founder-operated — which is why the second scorer and published inter-rater agreement are the first funded milestone rather than a someday item.
